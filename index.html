Section 01

Executive Summary

Gujarat has emerged as one of India's most consequential laboratories for urban ecological transformation under the Nagar Van Yojana — demonstrating that publicly funded green infrastructure, when designed with community at its centre, can simultaneously deliver ecological, economic, and psychological dividends to its citizens.

This State Impact Dossier presents a comprehensive assessment of the Nagar Van Yojana (NVY) in Gujarat, synthesising field evidence, secondary institutional data, and applied research frameworks to evaluate outcomes across four human-centric pillars: Public-Private Partnership (PPP) governance efficacy, socio-economic livelihood generation, psychological well-being (WHO-5), and ecological impact including Willingness to Pay (CVM).

Gujarat currently hosts one of the largest NVY portfolios in peninsular India, with sanctioned sites across major urban centres including Surat, Gandhinagar, Ahmedabad, Vadodara, Rajkot, and Jamnagar. The state's Forest and Environment Department, operating under the Principal Chief Conservator of Forests (PCCF), has demonstrated commendable institutional agility — deploying the e-GujForest FMIS application and the Transit Pass Management System (TPMS) as complementary digital governance tools that, while originally designed for wildlife and timber management, offer powerful templates for NVY monitoring integration.

The two primary research sites — Sultanabad/Suvali Nagar Van in Surat and the Indroda Nature Park / GEER Foundation area in Gandhinagar — represent a strategically valuable contrast: an emerging industrial-coastal urban forest tested under conditions of high pollution and economic pressure, and a mature, institutionally robust ecological hub serving millions of visitors annually. Together, they offer a near-complete picture of the scheme's potential across the urban development spectrum.

Section 02

Scheme Architecture, Budget & Components

The Nagar Van Yojana was launched on 5 June 2020 (World Environment Day) by the MoEFCC as a paradigm-shifting instrument to bring structured, biodiversity-rich urban forests to India's rapidly expanding cities. The scheme moved decisively beyond the earlier Nagar Van Udyan Yojana (2015) by embedding a mandatory Public-Private Partnership (PPP) framework, outcome-linked reporting requirements, and integration with the National Compensatory Afforestation Fund.

Modern Innovations & Digital Governance

Gujarat's Forest and Environment Department has positioned itself as a national leader in technology-driven conservation management. The institutional innovations developed primarily for wildlife management in the Gir landscape are directly transferable to the NVY monitoring architecture and represent a significant competitive advantage for the state's urban forestry programmes.

Gujarat's Digital Forest Infrastructure

e-GujForest FMIS

Android-based Forest Management Information System developed in Gujarati and English. Records forest offences, wildlife sightings, staff deployment, and plantation data in real-time. Directly deployable for NVY canopy and survival tracking.

Web-based digital platform for issuance of inter-state and intra-state transit permits for timber, bamboo, and minor forest produce. Ensures traceability of nursery-grown native species dispatched to NVY sites — closing the supply chain transparency gap.

Detailed Site Case Studies

The three sites profiled below were selected on the basis of strategic research value: they represent distinct typologies of urban forest development in Gujarat — an emerging industrial-coastal forest, a mature institutional ecological campus, and a brownfield-to-greenfield transformation — providing a robust comparative dataset for the fellowship's mixed-methods evaluation.

Surat, India's fifth-largest city and a globally significant diamond and textile manufacturing hub, faces acute Urban Heat Island (UHI) pressures and persistent air quality challenges. The Sultanabad/Suvali Nagar Van represents the scheme's most ambitious attempt to simultaneously deliver ecological resilience and coastal fringe ecosystem restoration in a high-density industrial corridor.

The centrepiece of this site is the adoption of the Miyawaki afforestation technique — a Japanese method that uses dense, multi-species native planting to compress decades of forest succession into 2–3 years. The plantation of over 5.5 lakh native trees across 4.50 hectares has created India's most data-rich urban Miyawaki forest under NVY, offering an unprecedented opportunity to measure both ecological and socio-economic outcomes in parallel.

The coastal proximity of the Suvali component makes this site uniquely valuable for measuring urban-coastal fringe ecosystem services, including wind attenuation, salt-spray mitigation, and habitat connectivity for coastal bird species.

Key Research Opportunities at This Site

  • CVM WTP survey for coastal green buffer services

Indroda Nature Park is India's most institutionally mature urban ecological campus and one of the country's most remarkable success stories in long-term urban forest restoration. Spanning 428 hectares on both banks of the Sabarmati River in Gandhinagar, the park was established through sustained planting efforts initiated by the Gujarat Forest Department in 1970 — and today stands as living proof that urban forests deliver multi-generational dividends.

Popularly known as "India's Jurassic Park", the park houses the country's only dinosaur museum, a zoological park, vast botanical gardens, an amphitheatre, an interpretation centre, and camping facilities. Its Wilderness Park component is home to hundreds of nilgai, langurs, peafowl, a diverse avifauna, and reptile populations — all thriving within a metropolitan capital city.

The park is managed by the Gujarat Ecological Education and Research (GEER) Foundation, an NAEB-affiliated body with deep institutional capacity for ecological research, monitoring, and community education — making it the ideal site for WHO-5 well-being surveys and CVM-based WTP estimation in a high-footfall urban forest context.

Jadeshwar Van in Ahmedabad represents arguably the most symbolically powerful NVY success story in Gujarat: the complete ecological transformation of a municipal solid waste dumping site into a functional, bio-diverse urban mini-forest. This brownfield-to-greenfield reversal directly addresses one of urban India's most intractable environmental governance failures — the co-location of contaminated wastelands and densely populated residential zones.

The Ahmedabad Municipal Corporation (AMC), in partnership with corporate CSR contributors, executed soil remediation, phytoremediation, and dense native plantation to convert what was once an environmental hazard into a community green space. This site is particularly significant for the fellowship's research as it demonstrates the circular economy dimensions of NVY: ecological restoration doubling as waste management, public health infrastructure, and real estate value enhancement for surrounding communities.

The site offers a compelling case study for SLF-based livelihood mapping, as the peripheral economy around the restored green space — including street vendors, chai stalls, morning walker groups, and informal nature guides — represents a measurable socio-economic dividend generated entirely by the scheme.

National Replication Potential

  • Model for 3,000+ municipal dumpsites across India
  • Phytoremediation cost benchmarking for PMU circulars
  • Peripheral economy mapping (SLF Five Capitals approach)
  • Before-after satellite imagery for NAEB national showcase
  • Community health outcomes comparison (pre vs. post restoration)

Pillar 1: Ecological & Biodiversity Impact

Environmental Resilience | Native Species | NDVI | UHI Mitigation

Gujarat's NVY sites — particularly the Miyawaki-method plantations in Surat — demonstrate measurable progress in canopy densification and native species biodiversity recovery. The multi-storeyed planting architecture of the Miyawaki technique (shrubs, sub-canopy, and canopy layers planted simultaneously at 3–5 trees per square metre) compresses natural forest succession timelines by 30–50 years, achieving a 40%+ canopy density target mandated by NVY guidelines within 2–3 seasons rather than decades.

Indroda Nature Park's long-term ecological trajectory — from barren riverbank to a 428-hectare wilderness supporting peafowl, nilgai, langurs, crocodiles, and over 200 bird species — provides the most compelling evidence in Gujarat (and arguably India) that sustained, institutionally supported urban afforestation delivers genuine biodiversity outcomes at scale.

Pillar 2: Socio-Economic Empowerment & Livelihood Generation

Sustainable Livelihoods Framework | Five Capitals | SLF Survey | Vendor Mapping

The Sustainable Livelihoods Framework (SLF) provides the analytical architecture for mapping livelihood capital assets — Human, Social, Natural, Physical, and Financial — that accrue to communities living within or adjacent to Nagar Van sites. In Gujarat, two distinct livelihood typologies are emerging around NVY sites, each demanding different policy responses:

(a) Peripheral Informal Economy: Around Indroda Park and Jadeshwar Van, a micro-economy of vendors selling refreshments, seasonal flowers, and handicrafts has developed organically around visitor footfall. This economy is entirely attributable to the NVY green space and represents a direct, measurable livelihood dividend — yet it remains entirely absent from current NAEB PMU quarterly reporting formats.

(b) Eco-Stewardship Employment: GEER Foundation's management of Indroda Park employs local naturalists, guides, and maintenance staff — a model of green institutional employment that can be systematically replicated at other mature NVY sites through PPP agreements with trained community forest management groups.

Pillar 3: Health & Psychological Well-being

WHO-5 Well-being Index | CVM (Willingness to Pay) | Mental Health Outcomes

The WHO-5 Well-Being Index — a validated, five-item psychometric scale scored out of 100 — will be administered to park visitors across both primary Gujarat sites to quantify the psychological dividend of urban green space access. International evidence consistently shows that access to urban forests is associated with reduced anxiety, improved mood, lower cortisol levels, and enhanced cognitive restoration — outcomes directly relevant to India's rapidly urbanising, stress-burdened working population.

The Contingent Valuation Method (CVM), implemented as a double-bounded dichotomous choice survey with an initial bid of ₹20 (branching to ₹50 or ₹10 based on response), will elicit Willingness to Pay values from Indroda Park visitors — providing the first-ever economic valuation of a Gujarat NVY site's psychological and recreational services. This data will directly support the case for sustainable self-financing mechanisms, such as enhanced entry fees, green premium memberships, and corporate wellness programme partnerships.

Pillar 4: Water Conservation & Climate Resilience

Soil Moisture | Water Harvesting | UHI Mitigation | NDC Contribution

Gujarat's semi-arid to arid climate — characterised by high inter-annual rainfall variability, intense monsoon concentration, and severe dry-season water stress — makes the water conservation components of NVY sites particularly valuable. The NVY guidelines mandate the construction of percolation ponds, check dams, and soil-moisture conservation structures that directly support groundwater recharge and local hydrology.

Indroda Park's riparian location along the Sabarmati River creates a natural green buffer that moderates riverbank erosion, filters urban runoff, and sustains riparian biodiversity. The park's forested edges function as a critical ecological service provider for Gandhinagar's urban water cycle — a function that has never been monetised or incorporated into the NAEB PMU's annual progress reports.

Gujarat's NVY portfolio directly contributes to India's updated NDC commitment to create an additional carbon sink of 2.5–3 billion tonnes of CO₂ equivalent through forest and tree cover, as enshrined in India's Paris Agreement obligations.

Section 06

Strategic Recommendations for the CEO, National CAMPA

The following three recommendations are grounded in Gujarat's demonstrated institutional strengths and field evidence. Each is immediately actionable, nationally scalable, and directly aligned with the National CAMPA's mandate under the CAF Act 2016. They are designed to shift the scheme's evaluation architecture from a sapling-survival paradigm to a community-resilience paradigm.

01

Mandate a Standardised Human-Centric KPI Module in All NVY Annual Reports

The current NAEB PMU reporting template focuses exclusively on physical and financial metrics — survival rates, fencing completed, funds utilised. Gujarat's Indroda Park and Jadeshwar Van demonstrate that the scheme generates substantial socio-economic, psychological, and ecological dividends that remain entirely invisible to policymakers because no standardised measurement protocol exists.

The National CAMPA should issue a revised reporting circular requiring all state NVY sites with mature vegetation (3+ years) to append a Human-Centric Outcomes Module to their annual progress reports. This module should include: (a) a WHO-5 Well-Being Index survey of 30–50 visitors, (b) an SLF peripheral economy census of vendors and community groups, (c) a one-page PPP depth assessment against a standardised rubric, and (d) a visitor footfall count with entry revenue statement. Gujarat's GEER Foundation should be commissioned to develop the template, given its existing research infrastructure and experience managing high-footfall urban ecological sites.

Implement via: Revised NAEB PMU Reporting Circular | Timeline: October 2026
02

Scale the Miyawaki-NVY Integration Protocol as a National Standard for Urban Forest Densification

Surat's Sultanabad/Suvali Nagar Van is currently among India's largest Miyawaki-method NVY plantations under the CAMPA framework. The technique's ability to establish a self-sustaining, multi-species native forest in 2–3 years — compared to 20–30 years under conventional plantation — directly addresses the scheme's most critical vulnerability: the gap between physical planting outputs and genuine ecological outcomes.

The National CAMPA should issue a Miyawaki-NVY Integration Technical Note that: (a) documents the Surat prototype's species composition, spacing, and survival methodology; (b) establishes NDVI-verified canopy density benchmarks at 1, 2, and 3 years post-plantation; (c) links Miyawaki adoption to preferential second-instalment fund release; and (d) identifies 25 high-UHI cities across India where this method should be prioritised. Gujarat SFD's e-GujForest FMIS application should be adapted to include a Miyawaki-specific monitoring layer for digital field validation.

Implement via: NAEB PMU Technical Note + APO Vetting Criteria | Timeline: August 2026
03

Establish the GEER Foundation as a National Centre of Excellence for Urban Forest Research Under CAMPA

The Gujarat Ecological Education and Research (GEER) Foundation represents the most institutionally capable, research-active, and ecologically credible urban forest management body in India — with over five decades of expertise managing one of the country's most complex urban ecological systems. Its institutional architecture, scientific staff, satellite monitoring capabilities, and public engagement programmes are precisely what the NVY scheme needs at the national level but currently lacks.

The National CAMPA should formally designate the GEER Foundation as a National Resource Centre for Urban Forestry Research (NRCUFR) under the scheme, with a mandate to: (a) develop and maintain the national NVY WebGIS dashboard with NDVI and PRANA data integration; (b) conduct biennial national assessments of NVY outcomes using the Human-Centric KPI framework; (c) provide capacity-building training to State Forest Department NVY nodal officers across India; and (d) establish a peer-reviewed Urban Forestry Research Journal under MoEFCC auspices. A dedicated annual CAMPA allocation of ₹5–10 crore should support this institutional strengthening.

Implement via: MoEFCC Office Order + CAMPA Fund Allocation | Timeline: March 2027
